Birch Bark Flatbread

The history of this flatbread can be traced back to the Viking Age. It was a time of conquest. It was a time of adventure. It was a time when our warriors needed simple sustenance such that it could keep us on our feet and allow us to withstand the rigors of long expeditions. A bread rolled thin and baked flat at a temperature to rival the forge of Mjöllnir is a dry and hard bread ideal for storage and transportation.

It is fascinating to see how the ancient bread has endured, while still maintaining its importance in everyday cuisine. Nowadays, it is a staple more often served with butter or cheese rather than making up a whole meal in and of itself. The version before you today incorporates birch bark, a variation borrowed from the Sami people of Lapland. Our northern brethren knew how to extract nutrition from the most unlikely of sources, and I present it here for you to enjoy now.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup of birch bark flour (can substitute with rolled oats)
  • 4 ¼ cups rye flour
  • 1 ½ teaspoons kosher salt, plus more salted cultured butter, melted

Directions

  1. In a large bowl, whisk together the oats, flour, and salt. Add enough water to bring together into a dough.
  2. Knead until smooth.
  3. Divide dough into 3 portions and flatten each portion into a paper-thin circle using a kruskavel.
  4. Cut out a circle in the middle of the bread so there is a hole
  5. Brush each flatbread with butter and sprinkle with a little salt.
  6. Heat the oven to 425.
  7. Bake on a greased baking pan for about 10 minutes.
